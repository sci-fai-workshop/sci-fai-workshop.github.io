NeurIPS 2026 · Workshop Proposal

Sci-fAI

Science for Artificial Intelligence
AI as a new object of science — connecting and inspiring multidisciplinary efforts across mathematics, physics, cognitive science, and neuroscience to reveal the principles inside foundation models.

Overview

This workshop convenes the scientific communities — spanning mathematics, physics, cognitive science, neuroscience, and beyond — that can advance the foundational understanding and design of modern AI systems.

AI was originally built from the foundational principles of many sciences, but contemporary model development has drifted into a regime where scaling, data curation, and empirical trial-and-error dominate, and the scientific traditions that seeded the field have receded from the design loop. While AI has powered discoveries across many sciences, researchers from a wide range of disciplines are now going in the inverse direction — pushing the new frontier of Science for AI that reveals the principles underlying today's standard practices, and turns theory into the next generation of systems.

Capabilities such as in-context learning and multi-step reasoning were never explicitly coded; they emerged. Macroscopic regularities like empirical scaling laws, double descent, grokking, contested phase-transition-like emergence, and structured circuit formation challenge our theories and intuitions about intelligence. Our relationship with AI has shifted from pure construction to a dialogue between discovery and design.

Treated as model organisms — probeable, perturbable, and comparable across scale and architecture — neural networks can be understood well enough to inform their own next iteration, with the rigor that mature engineering disciplines take for granted.

Contributing traditions include (but are not limited to) the mathematics of learning dynamics and generalization, the statistical physics of disordered learning systems, cognitive science approaches to behavioral and conceptual probing, and neuroscience methods for reverse-engineering opaque networks — together with adjacent fields such as information theory, dynamical systems, philosophy of science, and others that bring methodological depth to a science of AI.

Goals

  1. Map alignments and disagreements. Convene leading researchers across the four traditions to harmonize terminology and surface points of tension.
  2. Translate findings into design. Turn empirical findings about emergence, phase transitions, and circuit formation into actionable principles for new architectures, optimizers, and training regimes.
  3. Formalize standards for a science of AI. What counts as a valid explanation, a sound measurement, and an empirical law in this domain?
  4. Lower the barrier to entry. Welcome researchers from physics, mathematics, neuroscience, and cognitive science who hold the methodological tools but lack an entry point into mainstream ML.

Call for Contributed Work

We welcome empirical, theoretical, and position submissions that bring mathematics, physics, cognitive science, and neuroscience to bear on understanding and designing modern AI systems.

Format: Extended abstracts of up to 4 or 8 pages (excluding references) in the standard NeurIPS format. All accepted papers are non-archival to encourage ongoing work. Reviewing is conducted via OpenReview.

Selection & Awards: High-quality submissions will be selected for Contributed Talks (Spotlights) and will be eligible for the Best Paper Award.

Travel Subsidies & Inclusion: We will reserve travel subsidies for students, early-career researchers, and participants from underrepresented groups and from the natural-science communities we aim to draw in.

Topics of interest (not limited to)

Mathematics & Learning Theory

Learning dynamics, generalization theory, optimization geometry, random matrix theory, and provable guarantees for modern architectures.

Physics-inspired AI

Statistical mechanics of learning, phase transitions, mean-field and feature-learning limits, disordered systems, and macroscopic laws.

Cognitively-inspired AI

Behavioral paradigms, psychophysics-style probes, theory-of-mind evaluation, dual-process models, and cognitive primitives.

Neuroscience- & brain-inspired AI

Circuit-level analyses, ablation/perturbation methods, population-activity analysis, and architectural templates (e.g., attractor dynamics, predictive coding).

Mechanistic Interpretability

Reverse-engineering trained networks, circuit-motif discovery, causal validation, and cross-architecture comparison.

Position & Methodology

What should a science of AI look like? Evaluation standards, epistemology, and methodological foundations.
