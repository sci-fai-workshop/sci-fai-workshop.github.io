Sci-fAI

Science for Artificial Intelligence
AI as a new object of science — bringing the methods of mathematics, physics, cognitive science, neuroscience, and adjacent disciplines to bear on the principles inside foundation models.

Overview

Science for AI reverses the familiar “AI for Science” arrow: rather than using AI to study nature, it asks how the methods of every scientific tradition can be brought to bear on understanding — and ultimately designing — modern AI systems.

AI was originally built from the foundational principles of many sciences, but contemporary model development has drifted into a regime where scaling, data curation, and empirical trial-and-error dominate, and the scientific traditions that seeded the field have receded from the design loop. While AI has powered discoveries across the sciences, researchers from a wide range of disciplines are now going in the inverse direction — pushing the new frontier of a science of AI itself.

Capabilities such as in-context learning and multi-step reasoning were never explicitly coded; they emerged. Macroscopic regularities like empirical scaling laws, double descent, grokking, contested phase-transition-like emergence, and structured circuit formation challenge our theories and intuitions about intelligence. Our relationship with AI has shifted from pure construction to a dialogue between discovery and design.

Treated as model organisms — probeable, perturbable, and comparable across scale and architecture — neural networks can be understood well enough to inform their own next iteration, with the rigor that mature engineering disciplines take for granted.

Contributing traditions include (but are not limited to) the mathematics of learning dynamics and generalization, the statistical physics of disordered learning systems, cognitive science approaches to behavioral and conceptual probing, and neuroscience methods for reverse-engineering opaque networks — together with adjacent fields such as information theory, dynamical systems, philosophy of science, and others that bring methodological depth to a science of AI.

Why now?

Three pressures converge. Capability has outrun understanding. The tools to close the gap — statistical-mechanics analyses of training, learning-dynamics theory, mechanistic interpretability, and behavioral probing — have matured into falsifiable, transferable claims, yet remain siloed across communities. And agentic deployment sharpens the stakes: as models are handed tools, memory, and autonomy, failure modes such as reward hacking, deceptive alignment, and brittle out-of-distribution behavior become safety problems rather than benchmark curiosities, and post-hoc patching does not scale to systems we cannot predict.

Goals

  1. Map alignments and disagreements across scientific traditions — harmonize terminology and surface points of tension.
  2. Translate findings into design. Turn empirical findings about emergence, phase transitions, and circuit formation into actionable principles for new architectures, optimizers, and training regimes.
  3. Formalize standards for a science of AI. What counts as a valid explanation, a sound measurement, and an empirical law in this domain?
  4. Lower the barrier to entry for researchers who hold the methodological tools but lack an intuitive entry point into mainstream ML.

Science for AI is not an alternative to AI engineering — it is the science on which the next generation of AI engineering must rest.

Call for Papers

We welcome empirical, theoretical, and position submissions that bring any scientific tradition to bear on understanding and designing modern AI systems.

Format
Extended abstracts of 4 or 8 pages (excluding references) in the standard NeurIPS LaTeX format. Supplementary material is allowed and unreviewed.
Submission
Double-blind peer review via OpenReview. Link to be announced after workshop acceptance.
Archival
Non-archival. Accepted papers do not preclude future publication elsewhere. Work currently under review is welcome.
Awards
Top submissions selected as Contributed Talks (Spotlights). Eligible for the Best Paper Award.
Subsidies
Travel subsidies reserved for students, early-career researchers, and participants from underrepresented groups and adjacent natural-science communities.
Reviewing
Where possible, each submission is read by both an ML reviewer and a domain-science reviewer, so interdisciplinary work is judged on both axes.

Topics

We welcome submissions that advance a science of AI. Themes include, but are not limited to:

  • Understanding emergent capabilities: theoretical, empirical, and statistical-mechanical accounts of in-context learning, multi-step reasoning, scaling laws, grokking, phase-transition-like phenomena, and structured circuit formation.
  • Learning dynamics, generalization, and optimization: the mathematics of how networks learn, why they generalize, and the geometry and stability of training landscapes.
  • Networks as model organisms: controlled experiments on trained models — probing, ablation, perturbation, behavioral characterization, and cross-model comparison — drawing on tools from neuroscience, cognitive science, and ML.
  • Inductive biases and design templates from the natural sciences: brain-inspired architectures, attractor dynamics, predictive coding, statistical-physics priors, cognitive primitives, and other concrete imports into AI.
  • Foundations and methodology of a science of AI: what counts as a valid explanation, measurement, or empirical law in this domain? Evaluation standards, epistemology, and position pieces.
  • Adjacent traditions: information theory, dynamical systems, statistics, philosophy of science, linguistics, and other fields that bring methodological depth to the scientific study of AI.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the review process double-blind?
Yes — please anonymize your submission. Author names, affiliations, and self-identifying material in the body should be removed for review.
Will accepted papers be published in proceedings?
No. The workshop is non-archival. Accepted papers will appear on OpenReview and can be cited from there, but do not conflict with future publication elsewhere.
Can I submit work currently under review at another venue?
Yes. Concurrent submissions are welcome, provided they are on-topic. Please follow the originating venue's rules for parallel submission.
What format do accepted papers present in?
All accepted papers present as posters. Top submissions are also invited to give contributed talks (Spotlights).
Are virtual talks allowed?
We strongly prefer in-person attendance. Virtual talks may be allowed only in special circumstances (e.g., visa denials) or to materially increase the diversity of voices.
I'm from physics / math / cognitive science / neuroscience — is my work in scope?
Yes — strongly. The workshop is designed to draw native researchers from these traditions into a shared conversation about AI. Submissions that are methodologically rooted in your home discipline but speak to AI systems are exactly what we want.
How do I apply for a travel subsidy?
Subsidies will be coordinated alongside paper notifications. Details will appear here closer to the workshop date.
